# Stepping Stones — Product Concept

## The Gap

Interactive, pedagogically rigorous learning experiences — the kind Brilliant.org
has refined into an art form — require enormous human authorship effort. Every
lesson is hand-crafted: problems designed, visuals built, reasoning sequences
sequenced by domain experts. That effort produces something genuinely valuable:
active, engaging learning that meets a learner where they are conceptually and
pulls them forward. But the cost of that authorship means the content is
necessarily fixed, generic, and one-size-fits-all.

Meanwhile, AI tutoring tools (Khanmigo, Socratic, chat-based assistants) can
personalize in conversation — but what they produce is ephemeral. A tutoring
session evaporates. There is no artifact, nothing to share, nothing to revisit.
The cost of the interaction is paid once and discarded.

The gap is the intersection: a pedagogically rigorous, interactive learning
experience that is *also* bespoke to a specific learner's existing knowledge and
*also* a persistent, shareable artifact.

## The Value

Stepping Stones generates a personalized interactive lesson for any learner, on
any concept, tailored to where they already are.

A learner arrives with something they want to understand — a mathematical concept,
a scientific principle, a historical framework, a programming idea, anything. The
platform interviews them briefly to understand their existing knowledge: what they
already know, what analogies they think in, where their gaps are. It then
generates a lesson in the tradition of Brilliant.org — interactive problems,
visual reasoning, progressive guided discovery that requires active engagement
rather than passive reading — built specifically for that learner's baseline.

The result is not a conversation. It is a structured, interactive educational
artifact: something with a beginning, a middle, and an end; something that can be
opened again tomorrow; something that can be sent to a friend or distributed to a
classroom.

## Why Existing Tools Fall Short

**Fixed-content platforms** (Brilliant, Khan Academy) invest deeply in
pedagogical quality but cannot personalize to individual knowledge baselines.
Their content is authored for an assumed learner. A physicist trying to understand
market microstructure and a finance student trying to understand the same thing
need fundamentally different entry points — and no fixed library can serve both
well.

**AI tutoring tools** (Khanmigo, LLM chat interfaces) can adapt to a learner's
baseline in the moment but produce nothing durable. The personalization is
consumed in the interaction and then gone. There is no artifact to return to, no
lesson to share, no way to amortize the cost of a good explanation across multiple
learners who need it.

Neither camp solves the problem. Fixed content is high quality but generic.
Ephemeral tutoring is personalized but disposable. The structural constraint in
both cases is the same: human authorship at scale is expensive, and until
recently, there was no alternative.

## Why LLMs Are the Unblocker

Two things were previously only possible with expensive human authorship:

1. **Assessing a learner's actual baseline.** Understanding where a specific
   person already is — what concepts they hold, what analogies they reach for,
   what gaps exist just below the surface — requires open-ended dialogue and
   interpretation. LLMs can conduct that interview and synthesize a nuanced
   picture of a learner's knowledge state.

2. **Composing a bespoke pedagogical experience.** Writing a lesson that meets a
   specific learner at their specific baseline, in the tradition of active and
   interactive pedagogy, requires authoring judgment. LLMs can exercise that
   judgment — selecting the right analogies, the right problem sequence, the right
   level of abstraction — and compose it into a coherent experience from
   well-defined building blocks.

Together, these make per-learner authorship economically viable for the first
time. The generation cost is paid once; the resulting artifact can be used
indefinitely.

## Illustrative Use Cases

These are examples of how the platform might be used, not a definition of its
scope.

**The curious professional.** A software engineer reads a paper that references
Bayesian inference and realizes she doesn't actually understand it. She goes to
Stepping Stones, describes what she wants to learn, and answers a few questions
about her background. Ten minutes later she has an interactive lesson that builds
from her existing understanding of probability, uses examples from software
systems she already knows, and walks her through the core reasoning with guided
problems. She saves it, returns to it the next day, and forwards it to a colleague
who had the same gap.

**The professor.** A university instructor teaching an introductory economics
course wants students to engage with the concept of price elasticity before the
next lecture. Rather than assigning a textbook chapter, she uses Stepping Stones
to generate a lesson pitched at students with high-school math and no prior
economics. She shares the artifact with the class. Students work through it at
their own pace; the instructor doesn't need to author anything.

**The student on a deadline.** A graduate student hits a prerequisite concept he
never properly learned while working through a paper the night before a seminar.
He generates a lesson on the spot, works through it in forty minutes, and arrives
at the seminar able to participate.

## What Stepping Stones Is Not

Stepping Stones is not a tutoring chatbot — it produces structured artifacts, not
conversations. It is not a content library — nothing is authored in advance. It is
not a learning management system — it does not track progress across sessions or
maintain a model of the learner over time. Each lesson is self-contained.
